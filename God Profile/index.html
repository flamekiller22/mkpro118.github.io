The Temple of Apollo at Delphi
The Temple of Apollo at Delphi is an ancient Greek temple dedicated to the god Apollo. It is located on the slopes of Mount Parnassos in Delphi, Greece and is considered one of the most important religious sites in ancient Greece. The temple was a major center for the worship of Apollo, as well as a center for the Pythian Games, which was a musical and athletic competition that were held every four years.
According to ancient Greek mythology, it was believed that the god Zeus released two eagles, one to the east and one to the west, and the point at which they met marked the center of the world. This spot was identified as Delphi and a sanctuary was built there in honor of Apollo. The original temple at Delphi was believed to have been built in the 7th century BCE and was eventually replaced by a larger temple in the 4th century BCE.

The first book in Homer’s Iliad is a myth about how Achilles’ anger is causing a great deal of suffering to the Greeks. Achilles’s anger can be traced back to the wrath of Apollo on the Greeks.
Chryseis, a priestess in the temple of Apollo is kidnapped by Agamemnon. Chryses, a priest in the temple of Apollo and the father of Chryseis, offers Agamemnon a handsome ransom and the scepter of Apollo in exchange for the release of his daughter. While the rest of the Greeks unanimously accept Chryses’ offer, Agamemnon stubbornly refuses to release Chryseis, and fiercely send Chryses away, ordering him to never return.
Chryses, fearful of the King of Mycenae, obeys the word of Agamemnon. Without speaking another word, he departs Agamemnon’s ship and goes to the shore, where he starts praying to Apollo. Chryses prays to Apollo to avenge his tears by showering arrows on the ships of Agamemnon.
Apollo, who loves and respects Chryses, hears his prayers, and enraged by Chryses’ condition, bombards the Greeks with arrows for NINE days.
On the tenth day, Achilles, a mighty Greek warrior, calls the Greeks together to ask why Apollo was so angry and wants find a way to stop the God’s wrath. Calchas, a seer who had guided the Greeks to Troy, says that he knows the reason for Apollo's anger but is afraid to speak up as he knows that the reason would offend Agamemnon. Achilles promises to protect Calchas from Agamemnon, and Calchas reveals that the anger of Apollo was because of Agamemnon's refusal to release Chryseis.
Calchas advises the Greeks to release Chryseis without a fee or ransom, and send a holy hecatomb to Apollo to appease him. Achilles offers to provide the hecatomb; however, Agamemnon is unwilling to give Chryseis unless he gets Briseis from Achilles’ tent, which angers Achilles further. Before Achilles is able to act further on his anger, the Goddess Minerva intervenes and commands Achilles to not be violent with his sword, rather be violent with his words.
Achilles, whose goal was to end Apollo’s wrath, obeys Minerva, and allows Agamemnon to take Briseis from his camp. Agamemnon, in turn, releases Chryseis and provides the hecatomb to propitiate Apollo. He chooses a crew of twenty oarsmen, with Ulysses as their captain and charges them to return Chryseis to her father.
Chryses, receives his daughter gladly and arranges for the holy hecatomb to be offered to Apollo. He prays to Apollo to now stop the Greek’s suffering since he has been avenged and his daughter returned. Apollo hears Chryses’ prayers and ends the showering of arrows on the Greeks. Joyous on winning back the favor of Apollo, the Greeks worshipped Apollo with songs and hymns all day. Apollo was pleased to hear their joyous voices and sent them fair wind on their way home.
The Pythian Games
The Pythian Games were a quadrennial athletic and musical competition held in Ancient Greece in honor of Apollo, the god of music, poetry, prophecy, and healing. The games were held at Delphi, on the slopes of Mount Parnassus, near the famous Temple of Apollo at Delphi, where the Pythian Oracle was located.
The games were first held in the late 7th century BCE, when the temple of Apollo at Delphi was founded and continued until the 4th century CE. The Pythian Games were one of the four major Panhellenic Games, along with the Olympic, Isthmian, and Nemean Games, and were considered to be one of the most important cultural events in ancient Greece. They consisted of athletic events, such as foot races, wrestling, and chariot races, as well as musical and poetic competitions.
The winners of the games were awarded crowns of laurel, a symbol of victory and honor, and their names and achievements were recorded in inscriptions and poems.
